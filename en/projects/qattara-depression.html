Qattara Depression: a brilliant project or a crazy idea?

Assessment using GIS and Remote Sensing

The idea of developing the Qattara Depression is a classic example of a grandiose but still unrealized engineering project. The main concept is to use the elevation difference between the Mediterranean Sea and the Qattara Depression to generate electricity.



I was interested in examining this project using modern methods and technologies, using open data sources. The project is not funded by any third-party organizations and is exclusively a scientific development.


Geography

The Qattara Depression is located in northwestern Egypt, in the Libyan Desert, approximately 300 km west of Cairo and 76 km south of the Mediterranean coast.


Fig. 1. Location of the Qattara Depression

The project was actively developed in the 20th century. Below are the main researchers and their works on the Qattara Depression.

  • De Martino G (1973). The Qattara Depression. Technical report, Hydraulics Institute, Naples, Italy
  • Kelada M (2010). Global hyper saline power generation Qattara Depression potential. Fourteenth International Middle East Power Systems Conference, Cairo, Egypt
  • Elsayed Marwa Adel, Ismaeel Walaa S.E. (2019). Environmental assessment for major development projects: A case study “Qattara Depression” // Journal of Cleaner Production, Volume 215
  • Elite Capital & Co (2023). Agreement with Egyptian Group for International Trade and Consulting to conduct a new feasibility study for the Qattara Depression in Egypt

Goals and Objectives of the Study

  • To analyze various scenarios for the development of the Qattara Depression in Egypt
  • To assess the potential and possible risks of the project

Based on the Copernicus digital elevation model (30 m/px), a relief map of Egypt was constructed, highlighting the unique elevation position of the Qattara Depression.


Fig. 2. The depression is located below -50 m relative to the Mediterranean Sea

Geology

Using data from the Global Lithological Map Database, WHYMAP, U.S. Geological Survey's World Energy Project (WEP), and NOAA National Centers for Environmental Information, I created a geological map of Egypt. It can be noted that the Qattara Depression is largely composed of evaporites and is located in a zone of karst processes. Both of these factors are highly significant and are limiting factors in the development of the depression's exploitation project.


Fig. 3. Geological map of Egypt

Groundwater

Using WHYMAP data, I created a map reflecting the state of groundwater in Egypt. The Qattara Depression is partially located in a zone of saline groundwater and has small reserves of near-surface groundwater. It is worth noting separately that at depths exceeding 250 m, the groundwater reserves in this part of Africa are colossal.


Fig. 4. Groundwater of Egypt

Climate

Using WorldClim data, I compiled a selection of climate maps of Egypt, which clearly show that the depression has an extremely arid climate. Precipitation varies from 25 mm in the north, while the southern part receives no more than 10 mm. In summer, the average daytime air temperature is 36.2 °C, while in winter it drops to 6.2 °C. Prevailing winds blow from the north, with peak speeds (11.5 m/s) recorded in March and the lowest (3.2 m/s) in December.

NDVI

Google Earth Engine was used to calculate NDVI. I analyzed January Landsat mosaics for the period 1986-2025. January was chosen as the wettest month, and the mosaic was compiled to ensure cloud-free coverage of the territory. Analysis of the index distribution showed that vegetation is absent in the northern part of the Qattara Depression, while it appears periodically in the southern part.

Development Scenarios

I examined three scenarios for the development of the Qattara Depression.
In the first scenario, I based my analysis on the design characteristics considered during the 20th century, i.e., the creation of a reservoir filled to the -60 m level.
Scenario 2 analyzed filling the depression to the -90 m level.
Scenario 3 represents an integrated approach to developing the depression without creating a large water body.

Scenario 1

In the first scenario, I based my analysis on the design characteristics considered during the 20th century, i.e., the creation of a reservoir filled to the -60 m level. The pipeline length in this scenario is about 80 km, and a hydroelectric power plant is built at the boundary of the reservoir. The figure shows possible pipeline routes and the potential location of hydroelectric power plants.

The advantages of this approach include cheap energy, some climate mitigation, the creation of a transport hub, and the development of fisheries.

However, the disadvantages of this scenario are quite significant. They include ultra-high salinity of the reservoir, the formation of giant salt marshes, the occurrence of salt storms, activation of karst-suffosion processes and increased seismicity in the region. There will also be significant shoreline erosion, filtration and groundwater rise phenomena, and the destruction of important desert biocenoses.


Development of the Qattara Depression territory according to Scenario 1

Scenario 2

The second scenario considered the possibility of creating a reservoir up to the -90 m level relative to the Mediterranean Sea. The land area used is reduced from 12,000 km² to 3,500 km². The load on the earth's crust is reduced, as the water volume decreases from 228 km³ to 15 km³, and the project implementation time is reduced from 15 to 7 years. The advantages of this approach include cheap electricity generation, the creation of an aquatic ecosystem, salt production, fisheries, and the creation of a small recreation zone. However, there will be soil salinization, salt transfer, activation of karst-suffosion processes, shoreline erosion processes, groundwater rise, and destruction of biocenoses. The cost of the project increases significantly due to the lengthening of the pipeline from 85 to 200-250 km. However, it becomes possible to install two hydroelectric power plants at the -60 and -90 m drops. The figure shows possible pipeline routes and the potential location of hydroelectric power plants.


Development of the Qattara Depression territory according to Scenario 2

Scenario 3

Scenario 3 considers not flooding, but high-tech industrial and recreational development of the Qattara Depression on an area of less than 1% of the depression (140 km²). Water from the Mediterranean Sea is pumped to the coastal ridge (+200 m) using pumps powered by solar energy. Part of the water goes to desalination and further water supply for the resort area. The main flow is directed to a cascade of evaporation ponds (140 km²) for the production of salt and valuable components (magnesium, bromine, potassium), also partially providing the resort complex with salt water. Construction works taking into account geological risks, including the use of geomembranes, anti-corrosion materials, and continuous karst monitoring, allow minimizing possible project risks. The advantages of this scenario include increasing Egypt's water security, creating a large number of jobs, the emergence of a major export sector of the economy, and the creation of a world-class resort.

The project is technically feasible, economically profitable (salt pays for the base in 3 years) and has strategic importance for Egypt. Instead of flooding the depression - smart industrial development working for the benefit of the country.

Conclusions

✓ Egypt possesses a unique territory with enormous potential for economic transformation

✓ The research shows that modern technologies can turn it into a profitable investment project

✓ The most promising approach is the integrated development of the Qattara Depression as an industrial and recreational cluster, combining salt production, water desalination, and world-class tourism infrastructure

What's Next?

Further development of the project requires additional geological, hydrogeological, environmental, and economic studies of the territory.
